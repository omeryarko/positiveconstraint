Positive Constraint

The world is too complex to predict and too fast to adapt to. Resilience can no longer be found in predictions or in moving faster. But there is a way forward. constraints resilience strategy abstraction intuition desired-reality 20 min read

The world is too complex to predict and too fast to adapt to. Resilience can no longer be found in predictions or in moving faster. But there is a way forward. The path to resilience will be found not in what's changing, but in what's not. And while everyone rushes to AI, finding it takes a human skill that machines can't replicate.

Complex New World

If you also feel that the world has gone mad, spinning out of control, you're not alone. It has. You're right.

Over the last few years alone, we lived through a global pandemic, wars, supply-chain collapse, an energy crisis, economic whiplash, and the rise of AI. Not one at a time. Everything, everywhere, all at once. COVID hit global supply chains, which spiked inflation, which reshuffled geopolitics from Ukraine to Israel to Hormuz and Taiwan.

This is what experts call a polycrisis. Not a sequence of separate problems, but a web where each one deepens the others.

And it's not slowing down. At the time of this writing, scientists warn that another global pandemic is a matter of when, not if. Weather models are forecasting what could be the strongest El Niño in 140 years, driving famine and mass migration. Meanwhile, the conflict around Taiwan threatens to reach a climax around 2027, sending global powers to each other's throats. And energy prices, spiked by the Hormuz conflict, show no sign of coming down. Each crisis feeding the next. Each adding fuel to the fire.

But even without a single crisis, our world would still be changing fast. Technology alone would see to that.

In 2016, one analysis listed 73 ways driverless cars will change our lives. People will know exactly when they need to leave and when they will arrive. Parents will trust a secure service with their children. Without the burden of car ownership, how we spend and save money will change. Where we live, how our cities look, and how we spend our time will all change. Seventy-three implications, from one technology.

And that's just driverless cars. Every aspect of our lives is going through its own disruption. Longevity is changing how long we live and how well we age. How we grow and consume food is being reinvented. How we generate and store energy. We're venturing into space and plan to settle on Mars. And then there's AI, of course, upending everything it touches.

Each of these will reshape our lives as deeply as driverless cars, sending its own ripples throughout society. Predicting how one might change our lives is possible, in isolation. But these disruptions are not happening in isolation. They are all happening at the same time, in one big turbulent pool. Predicting how all these crises and disruptions will affect us is impossible.

For the first time ever, as historian Yuval Noah Harari says, we have no idea what our world will look like in 20 years. A thousand years ago, we taught our kids how to shoot a bow or how to pick mushrooms in the forest. A hundred years ago, we encouraged them to become doctors, lawyers, or engineers to make the most of the new world. The very basic aspects of life were predictable. Not anymore.

"If the rate of change on the outside exceeds the rate of change on the inside, the end is near."

— Jack Welch

If we can't predict the future, perhaps we can ride its wave instead. Adapting to change is what we've always done. It makes sense. As Jack Welch, one of the sharpest business minds of the 20th century, put it, outchange your environment, or die. And for a long time, it worked.

It doesn't work anymore.

Adapting assumes you can outchange your environment. But how do you outchange a world that moves faster than you do? By the time you adapt to your new reality, the world will change twice over.

It doesn't mean you shouldn't try. But constantly changing, riding wave after wave, is exhausting and unsustainable. People and organizations can only take so much before being overwhelmed by even faster and bigger changes.

For the first time in human history, the world is too complex to predict and too fast to adapt to. What used to work no longer does. We need a different strategy for this complex new world.

Embrace the Unchanging

When asked what's going to change in the next 10 years, Jeff Bezos said he's hardly ever asked what isn't going to change. For him that's the more interesting question, because you can build a strategy around the things that are stable in time.

No matter the state of the economy or the identity of the president, says Bezos, people will want low prices, vast selection, and fast delivery. When you know something is true even over the long term, you can afford to put a lot of energy into it. To this day, these three elements make up the core of Amazon's strategy and identity.

Instead of trying to predict what's next or to change faster than the world, Bezos anchored Amazon to what doesn't change.

Until now, we dealt with change by weathering the storm and waiting for it to pass before we could rise again. We built for the calm after the ripples. Now we can't. Now there is no calm. It's a turbulence of change.

We can no longer afford to wait for the calm that isn't coming. We need to build for the turbulence that is here to stay. And that means we stop chasing what's changing and anchor to what isn't.

But how do you find these unchanging elements? And how do you know they're the right ones for you?

At any given moment, there is an infinite number of things around you that do not change. The sun rises in the east and sets in the west. Gravity pulls you towards the earth. The global population is growing. Unchanging elements are everywhere you look. Most of them are irrelevant and hold no value to you. But a few could be the foundation you build your entire strategy around.

How do you know which elements are right for you? With the power of the human brain.

Copying someone else's is tempting. You could build around Amazon's three elements, but Amazon already owns them. The elements that anchor you need to be yours.

Machines can't help you find your elements. Machines are great at logic and data. AI excels at looking at what is known. But making sense of this complex new world is different. Nothing like this complexity has existed before. This is uncharted territory for humans and machines alike. There is no recorded data, no recognized patterns, nothing for machines to draw on or learn from. Spotting the right unchanging elements for you requires something machines don't have.

Bezos built around market truths. But unchanging elements can also come from a meaning you offer the world. Virgilio Martinez, one of the world's top chefs, wanted to serve Peru's true identity on a plate. Not just its cuisine, but what the country truly is. The answer came to him when he traveled the land, researching ingredients and communities, and learned of an Andean philosophy that sees the world differently. Not horizontally, but vertically, in levels and altitudes.

Peru's true identity, Martinez realized, reveals itself through its altitude. From the sea to the mountaintops, each level with its own ingredients, knowledge, and culture. An unchanging truth that his entire menu was built on.

Martinez didn't find altitude in a dataset. He used his intuition and creativity instead. Thomas Campbell, a former NASA physicist who has spent decades studying how the mind processes information, argues that intuition is more powerful than intellect. "The questions and the things we want to know that are really important", says Campbell, "there's no data that you can put in to come to a logical conclusion." Where data isn't available, the mind can intuit the way forward. But machines don't have intuition. They are like "a thousand left brains". Finding your unchanging elements isn't a left-brain problem, it's a right-brain one. And that is something only humans can do. It's how Martinez connected altitude, identity, and ingredients.

But how do we tap this intuition? How do we find our own unchanging elements?

Seeing Reality Anew

Constraints

The answer is constraints. And not in the way most people think of them.

Most people flinch from constraints. That's because they miss the point entirely. Artists, for example, have praised them for centuries. Yet what most people see as heavy restrictions might hold the answer we're looking for.

Artists rely on constraints for creativity. Leonardo da Vinci said that "art lives from constraints and dies from freedom". Best-selling author Umberto Eco adds that "constraints are fundamental to every artistic endeavor".

"In order to enable the story to proceed, the writer must impose some constraints. Constraints are fundamental to every artistic endeavor. A painter who decides to use oils rather than tempera, a canvas rather than a wall; a composer who opts for a given key; a poet who chooses to use rhyming couplets, or hendecasyllables rather than alexandrines—all establish a system of constraints. So too do avant-garde artists, who seem to avoid constraints; they simply construct others, which go unnoticed."

— Umberto Eco

But artists aren't the only ones who appreciate a good constraint. People in business, technology, medicine, education, and sports use constraints to sharpen their thinking. David Epstein shows this in his book "Inside the Box: How Constraints Make Us Better", and goes on to connect it to how the brain actually works. Our brains naturally seek the path of least resistance. Given infinite options, we fall back on familiar, unoriginal habits. But when constrained, the brain can't take shortcuts. It's forced to find new connections and solve problems differently. Not despite the constraint, but because of it.

Constraints don't limit the mind. They activate it.

This is where humans do something machines can't. AI excels at logic, patterns, and data. But constraints unlock access to a form of human intelligence that machines can't replicate. Rick Rubin, one of the most celebrated record producers alive, calls it conjuring. To Rubin, constraints don't force answers. They summon intuitive, non-linear creative leaps that have no words yet. Sculptor and philosopher Stanisław Szukalski refers to the creative process as perceiving the as yet unnamed.

If constraints can tap that power, could they be used to find the resilience we're after?

Tell your brain "you have three months and half the money" and it gets to work. But what constraints would lead Chef Martinez to Altitude? What would you feed your brain to conjure the foundation your strategy needs?

Constraints unlock the brain's power, but the complexity around you, your environment, and your challenge don't come neatly packed in constraints.

You need to translate everything to constraints so you can feed it to your brain and tap its power to find answers.

But how do you express the world in constraints?

Abstraction

Taking complex subjects and simplifying them to abstract forms is a skill artists perfect over years. To find the abstract form of a bull, Picasso looked for the few details that carry the most meaning. How much could he take away and still see a bull? Layer by layer he stripped it away, until nothing was left but a single line.

But it's not the same bull for everyone. It carries different meanings to different people in different contexts. A rancher doesn't care about the artistic line, but about the bull's lineage, age, and fertility. A child doesn't care about the bull's breeding value, she only sees a 'boy cow' that goes 'moo'.

Abstraction is the process of removing what's unimportant to reveal what's important, and what's important depends on the context.

Abstraction isn't something you need to learn. You were born with it. As the world grew more complex, people were forced to abstract their world to make sense of it. Psychologist James Flynn found that over the 20th century, abstract reasoning scores rose steadily across every population studied, a phenomenon now known as the Flynn Effect. We evolved to abstract the world around us.

If we could abstract the world into constraints, we'd be able to bridge the gap and feed the brain exactly what it needs to work with.

But what is a constraint, in this context? Not a limitation. Not a problem to solve. A constraint is an unchanging element that shapes how its environment changes.

Budget and time constraints are simple examples. A budget constraint is an unchanging element that shapes how money is spent. There are plenty of ways to spend a $100 budget, but that $100 is fixed. A time constraint is an unchanging element that shapes how time is spent. There are plenty of different outcomes to a 3-hour test or a 15-minute pitch.

Gravity is a constraint. It doesn't change, yet everything on Earth is shaped by it. The altitude of Peru is a constraint. It shaped the land, the food, the people, and ultimately the menu of one of the world's greatest restaurants. Amazon's low prices, vast selection, and fast delivery that customers want are constraints. They don't change, but they shape how Amazon will grow and change.

A nation's laws are constraints, guiding the behavior of its residents. The rules of a game shape how it will play out. Cultural traditions influence how people spend their weekends and holidays. Once you start seeing them, they're hard to miss.

You start seeing the world in constraints. It becomes a language. You see the forces that shape markets, values that guide decisions, necessities that leave no choice. Force. Shape. Influence. Values. Necessity. They all point to something that doesn't change with the power to shape everything around it.

So how do we abstract into constraints? Abstraction needs context that shows what to keep and what to remove. When abstracting into constraints, the context is what doesn't change. We remove what changes and keep what doesn't.

And by removing what changes to find what doesn't, you can abstract your world into constraints.

But something is still missing.

Desired Reality

Every strategy needs a goal, and a strategy for resilience is no different. "Find your unchanging elements" isn't a goal if it's shared by everyone. The goal needs to be yours.

A goal in the language of constraints is called a Desired Reality, and it does two things.

First, it gives the brain a constraint to work with. A problem described as pain or frustration gives the brain nothing to grip. When Martinez was still searching for his restaurant's identity, he started with a problem. His dishes didn't represent true Peru. But a problem describes a gap, not a destination. When he reframed it as a desired reality, to serve Peru's true identity on a plate, he had something to build around. An unchange he wished to manifest, express, and create.

A desired reality is not a vision. A vision for Martinez might have been 'create the next step for Peruvian cuisine' or 'communicate Peru in a way people have never seen before'. Those point to ambition but tell you nothing about what the world looks like when you get there. Serving Peru's true identity on a plate is concrete enough to act on. Ingredients from every altitude, heritage crops that shaped the people and the land, and flavors you can't find anywhere else.

The second thing a desired reality gives you is the context you need for abstracting the world. What you're building toward is the filter that shows what matters and what doesn't. In the process, you spot which unchanging elements have the power to shape your environment, and ignore everything else.

Constraints Mapping & Core Constraints

Abstracting the environment in the context of a desired reality is called Constraints Mapping. A few constraints will always matter more than the rest, and this process reveals them. These are the Core Constraints, the high-leverage constraints that have the power to bring about the desired reality.

There are many unchanging truths about Peru. Its altitude, its colonial architecture, the warmth of its people. They are all part of its identity. But only altitude tied together the land, the ingredients, and the culture of every level and ecosystem in a way that could be served on a plate. Altitude was the core constraint.

These core constraints are what we've set out to find. The key to your resilience.

Summary

The last few years weren't easy. They were overwhelming, turbulent, and scary. And if that's not enough, the global crises and technological disruptions aren't going anywhere. This age of rapid change is here to stay.

People and organizations alike are craving certainty. They struggle to find meaning in a world that stopped playing by the rules they grew up with. What used to work no longer does.

The paradox is that while people cling to what feels safe, the answer won't come from what they already know. It means stepping outside what's familiar to see reality anew and find the path forward.

Looking at the world through the lens of constraints allows us to see what's possible and what isn't. What can happen and what cannot. It's a way to see beyond what changes to what doesn't, and to build on firm ground.

This human skill lets you see past the surface into what drives the change itself. Not the noise, but what's underneath it. That's where certainty lives, even in a world that offers very little of it.

Wherever you look, there is complexity that constraints can cut through. Once you train yourself to see them, you start noticing the threads of unchange running through everything.

Your world will keep changing. It won't slow down for you, won't wait for you to catch up, and won't tell you where it's going.

What's your constraint?
